Let's define a few things before continuing:

## Sex

For this article I'll define sex as biological, the genitalia you have, whilst we could use chromosomes, a person can change sex or even be born with the opposite chromosomes so I don't think they're the optimal measure when we're not talking about medicine, rather its relation to gender.

## Gender

Gender is the societal role you're expected to fulfil, it could be said this is based on your sex but I'm not sure this is necessarily the case given that there aren't only 2 genders, even if it's often the case. I believe after reading this book that there are 3 possible groups of genders. Masculine, Feminine and Non-Binary, the latter of which likely has many sub-genders, e.g Two-Spirit. My argument for this is that Masculinity and Femininity are the gender expectations based on your sex and Non-Binary is everything else.

# In we dive.

In the book David D. Gilmore finds that the vast majority of societies around the world have the 2 traditional genders, masculine and feminine, with a few also having other roles.

The (general) masculine societal traits and expectations could be summed up as:
- Hunting animals
- Defend, either from other competing societies or predators
- Provide for your community (Meaning you provide more calories than you consume)
- Reinforce your manhood (the methods differ greatly across cultures)
- Maintain tools and weaponry
- War (Doesn't have to be WW2 scale, it can be as small as fights with other groups)

The (general) feminine societal traits and expectations can be summed up as:
- Gather
- Raise children
- Prepare and cook food
- Create and maintain clothing

# The Shift

A quite fantastic takeaway from the book I find is that societies marked by abundance, that is, societies in which food is more plentiful, there is no war or competition, the overlap of genders can sometimes overlap completely. (see the outliers section 8). So it would seem that the genders and expectations are strongly correlated to environment, NOT biology. I think too that that would generally put the two genders and their expectations of one another in sync as they both exist in the same exact same environment.

Our current society has 2 quite important changes to it, which I think throw off this synchronisation. (It's worth noting I have 0 evidence of this and I think getting this would be hard)

1. Pensions / Cost of living
2. Women making up a large portion of the workforce

Neither of these things prevent having children, but they do reduce the necessity of it.

### 1. Pensions

In most of human history children are a necessity, they perpetuate your society, and they look after you in your old age, when you are unable to do as much work as you used to be able to. Pensions (Which I think are a good thing) reduce this necessity as you can stash money away for later. Sure, since the invention of money you _could_ have done this, but I believe state pensions along with your work pension reduce this necessity massively.

### 2. Women Working

I think being able to get a job and be paid in a currency that can be used for goods and services, not needing to farm, or hunt or gather somewhat disintegrates the environmental need for gender roles, certainly as more and more women entered the workforce. As of writing it is roughly 50/50 in Canada (cite stat can). And now that this is 50/50, despite gender pay gaps, women and men can mostly afford to live single lives. (Of course room mates are very common but they aren't mating in quite the same way).

I believe women have largely accepted this diminishing demand for gender roles, and even, fought for it, true equality would mean no gender roles. And so women do not need a fully-affirmed masculine man who will provide, protect, hunt etc. Men are arguably optional and can be replaced with a vibrator and a sword. (Both of which are also much more reliable at their jobs).
